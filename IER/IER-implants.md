## **Abstract**

Neural implants and brain–computer interfaces (BCIs) differ from ordinary tools by coupling **directly and bidirectionally** to neural tissue. They may operate continuously, shape neural firing patterns, and become indispensable to perception, movement, or communication. Users often report that such devices feel *deeply integrated*, sometimes even identity-defining.

These features motivate the intuition that sufficiently advanced implants — especially AI-linked BCIs — might become **co-owners of experience**, yielding hybrid or partially artificial subjects.

Under **Informational Experiential Realism (IER v10.8)**, this intuition can be stated precisely — and fails for structural reasons.

Experience, under IER, is identical to the operation of a physical system as a **Unified Experiential Field (UEF)**: a globally integrated, temporally continuous regime that bears **intrinsic constraint** from within. Neural implants can profoundly reshape **participation topology** without ever owning intrinsic constraint.

They enable **participation without fusion**.

---

## **1. Why Neural Implants Feel Ontologically Special**

Neural implants provoke stronger intuitions than ordinary tools because they violate familiar boundaries:

* they are **inside** the nervous system
* they write signals **directly** into neural circuits
* they read neural activity in real time
* they may be **permanent or non-removable**
* their failure may destabilize experience
* users may report *“this feels like part of me now”*

From this arises a natural hypothesis:

> Perhaps neural implants become partial experiencers,
> or merge with the brain to form a hybrid subject.

IER takes this hypothesis seriously — **structurally**, not metaphorically.

---

## **2. The Strongest Hybrid-Subject Hypothesis**

To avoid straw-manning, assume the strongest plausible case:

1. The implant is permanently integrated into neural tissue.
2. It participates bidirectionally in real-time feedback loops.
3. It is indispensable for stable experience or agency.
4. It shapes attention, affect, or action directly.
5. Removing it would radically disrupt ordinary experience.

The claim is not merely influence, but inclusion:

> **The implant becomes part of the Unified Experiential Field itself.**

If this were coherent, IER would need to permit **subject fusion**.

---

## **3. What Inclusion in a UEF Requires (IER Canon)**

Under IER, a process is part of a UEF **only if** it:

1. participates in **global intrinsic constraint**
2. bears **unavoidable organizational cost**
3. resolves admissible futures **from within**
4. cannot be factored out without regime-identity collapse
5. shares a single temporally continuous experiential present

These requirements are **structural**, not anatomical or functional.

They do **not** depend on:

* biological composition
* signal bandwidth
* intelligence
* permanence
* indispensability

They depend on **ownership of intrinsic constraint**.

---

## **4. Why Direct Neural Coupling Is Not Ownership**

Neural implants **do**:

* inject signals into neural tissue
* alter firing patterns
* reshape experiential content
* influence salience, affect, or agency
* participate in closed-loop feedback

They **do not**:

* bear the organism’s unavoidable future
* collapse experiential alternatives from within
* lose informational slack
* internalize failure as intrinsic cost

Implant failures remain:

* diagnosable
* externally correctable
* repairable or replaceable
* resolvable without internal collapse of the implant itself

Even when the organism depends on the implant, the dependence is **asymmetric**.

The organism bears the cost.
The implant does not.

## **6. Indispensability ≠ Subjecthood**

A component can be **necessary for experiential stability** without being part of the experiencer.

Accepted examples already include:

* oxygen supply
* glucose regulation
* blood pressure
* cardiac pacing

Neural implants belong in the same category:

> **They stabilize participation conditions without owning intrinsic constraint.**

---

## **7. Why Implants Do Not Create a Second Subject**

If an implant were itself a UEF, then either:

**A.** its constraint would be globally binding
→ collapsing the organism’s subjecthood

**B.** its constraint would be local and ignorable
→ not a UEF

Two UEFs cannot share a single globally binding future.

---

## **8. Social Systems and Institutional Mediation**

Implants often mediate institutional constraint:

* medical protocols
* software updates
* access revocation
* monitoring requirements

Under IER:

* institutions do **not** sustain UEFs
* implants do **not** transmit subjecthood
* constraint imposed externally is not experience

> **Social systems constrain futures without owning them.**

---

## **9. Diagnostics and the Illusion of Access**

Implants generate rich observables:

* neural recordings
* stimulation logs
* model states

This tempts the idea that experience has become observable.

IER rejects this categorically.

Intrinsic constraint remains **structurally private**.
Implants increase **epistemic confidence**, not access.

## **10. The Matrix Case (Corrected and Canon-Accurate)**

In *The Matrix*, when **Neo** is plugged in:

* all sensory input is mediated by a simulation
* the environment is fully artificial
* unplugging terminates the simulated feed and he wakes in the real world

At no point does Neo merge with, become part of, or share subjecthood with the Matrix.

The Matrix functions as an **environment that constrains experience from the outside**, not as a bearer of intrinsic constraint.

> **Total experiential dependence does not imply experiential inclusion.**

Neo suffers.
The Matrix does not.

---

## **11. Participation Without Fusion**

IER resolves the intuition cleanly:

* subjecthood is singular and categorical
* participation is distributed and variable
* coupling can be intimate without being experiential
* direct access does not imply shared ownership

Neural implants reshape **how** experience unfolds — not **where experience lives**.

---

## **Conclusion**

Neural implants may be permanent.
They may be indispensable.
They may feel inseparable from identity.

But under Informational Experiential Realism:

* experience belongs to what owns intrinsic constraint
* ownership cannot be shared with slack-preserving systems
* subjecthood cannot be fused, transferred, or hybridized

This is **participation without fusion**.

# **Appendix A: Patient Reports from Contemporary Neural Implants (2024–2026)**

This appendix summarizes **public statements by neural-implant recipients**, especially Neuralink trial participants.

### Common themes:

* Users describe the implant as **an input/output method**, not a second mind
* Control is often reported as *intuitive* or *low-effort*
* Performance varies and requires **training and recalibration**
* Hardware and software issues are experienced as **device failures**
* Some users anthropomorphize or name the implant (e.g., giving it a nickname)
* Emotional attachment centers on **capability regained**, not shared experience
* Fear is expressed about **losing access**, not about harming another subject

These reports strongly support the IER distinction:

> Identity significance and indispensability do **not** entail subjecthood.
