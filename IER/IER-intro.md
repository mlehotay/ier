## Purpose

This essay is an **on-ramp**.

Its sole aim is to make one structural distinction intelligible **before formalization**:

> **Where the cost of resolution lives in a physical system.**

It does not argue for IER, define experience, or defend any downstream claim.
It identifies a **location** where later claims could plausibly attach, if they attach anywhere at all.

No agreement is required.
Only intelligibility.

## **Where Constraint Lives**

### I. Constraint Is Cheap — Until It Isn’t

Constraint is everywhere.

Any system that persists over time operates within bounds. Temperatures stay within ranges. Forces counteract one another. Inputs are regulated so outputs remain stable. None of this is exotic. Most engineered and biological systems are designed precisely to make constraint *cheap* — to ensure deviations are corrected quickly, locally, and without lasting consequence.

A thermostat is the simplest example. Temperature drifts, a mechanism responds, and the drift is corrected. Constraint exists, but nothing accumulates. A brief malfunction is absorbed; a total failure leaves the larger environment unchanged. The constraint never becomes a defining feature of the system’s future.

More complex control systems behave similarly. Feedback loops compensate for deviation. Error-correction restores expected states. Local failures are isolated. The system continues largely unchanged by the fact that something went wrong.

In these cases, constraint does not *belong* to the system in any deep sense. It is something the system manages, not something it must carry forward. Resolution happens, but it leaves no lasting mark on the organization as a whole.

At this level, constraint explains very little. It is a background condition of stability, not a source of significance.

---

### II. When Resolution Can Be Passed Along

As systems grow more complex, constraint management becomes more sophisticated.

Distributed control systems monitor many variables at once. Subsystems coordinate to maintain stability. When one component struggles, others compensate. Load is rerouted. Buffers absorb shock. Supervisory processes intervene when local mechanisms fail.

These systems can be remarkably robust. They may tolerate disruption without collapsing and even reconfigure in response to repeated failures. But one feature remains constant: **resolution can be passed along**.

When something goes wrong, there is somewhere else for the problem to go.

A subsystem is reset. A controller overrides a local process. A buffer delays impact until conditions improve. Even centrally made decisions can often be revised, reversed, or insulated. The system survives because no single resolution binds everywhere at once.

What matters here is not intelligence, complexity, or coordination. Highly complex systems can predict, optimize, and adapt while keeping alternatives open. Resolution occurs, but it does not force the system as a whole to reorganize irreversibly around that resolution.

The system handles problems, but it is not *changed by having handled them*.

---

### III. A Different Kind of Difficulty

Not all systems have this luxury.

Some systems encounter situations in which problems cannot be passed along, deferred, or isolated. There is no subsystem that can take over, no buffer that can absorb the cost, no external mechanism that can decide on the system’s behalf.

When resolution occurs in such systems, it does not happen *somewhere*. It happens **everywhere at once**, in the only sense that matters: the system’s future is reorganized as a whole.

Whatever happens next is not merely selected from a menu of options. It becomes the system’s future by being lived. Alternatives close, not because a controller chose among them, but because the system cannot avoid the consequences of how things unfold.

This kind of difficulty feels different, even when described in purely physical terms. The past now matters because it has narrowed what can happen next. The present matters because it is where alternatives collapse. The future matters because there is no way to undo that collapse without changing what the system is.

Nothing mystical has occurred. No new ingredients have been added. The laws governing the system remain the same. What has changed is simpler: the system can no longer treat resolution as something that happens *to* it.

Resolution has become something the system must live with, all at once.

---

### IV. The Question Sharpens: Who Bears the Cost?

At this point, the difference between systems can be stated more precisely.

Some systems resolve problems *about* themselves. Others resolve problems *as* themselves.

In the first case, resolution is detachable. Decisions can be evaluated, revised, or overridden without forcing a system-wide commitment. The system continues in roughly the same form, regardless of how often resolution occurs.

In the second case, there is no such insulation. Resolution is not a separate operation. Whatever happens next reshapes the system’s future everywhere at once. The system does not merely process the outcome; it carries it forward as a defining feature of what it can become.

This difference is not about who decides, how fast resolution occurs, or how much information is involved. It is about **where the cost of resolution lives**.

In many systems, cost can be exported. A failure can be isolated. A mistake can be undone. An external controller can absorb the consequences. Even serious errors need not reorganize the system as a whole.

In other systems, cost cannot be exported. When resolution occurs, the cost is borne internally. There is no stepping outside the outcome after the fact. What has been resolved becomes part of the system’s continuing structure.

The system does not just register the result. It *is altered by it*.

---

### V. Unavoidability Without Drama

This brings us to a careful sense of *unavoidability*.

Unavoidable does not mean predetermined, inevitable in advance, or lacking alternatives. In many cases, alternatives were genuinely available up to the moment of resolution.

What unavoidability refers to here is narrower: **once resolution occurs, there is no way for the system to avoid bearing its consequences**.

Some systems can evaluate futures without committing to them. They can simulate outcomes, compare options, and revise plans without closing off alternatives. In such systems, narrowing is provisional. Futures can reopen.

Other systems cannot do this. For them, narrowing *is* the consequence. The closing of alternatives is not a side effect of resolution; it is what resolution consists in. Once things go one way rather than another, there is no external reset, no supervisory undo, no neutral vantage point from which the outcome is merely informational.

This is not a claim about choice, freedom, or agency. It is a claim about organization over time. The system’s future becomes constrained by what has already happened, and that constraint is carried forward internally rather than absorbed elsewhere.

Nothing dramatic needs to be added. The system remains governed by the same physical laws and may still be predictable or controllable in many respects. The only change is that resolution has become something the system must live with, rather than something it can merely process.

At this point, the shape of the distinction should be visible, even if its later use is not yet clear.

---

### VI. Naming the Pattern (Gently, Once)

Up to this point, nothing hinges on terminology. The distinction can be followed without labels, definitions, or technical language. Still, it is useful to have a way of referring back to the pattern just described.

We can describe situations of this kind as involving **globally unavoidable internal constraint**.

The phrase is meant to be literal.

* *Global* means that resolution binds the system’s future as a whole, rather than remaining confined to a part.
* *Internal* means that the cost of resolution is borne within the system, rather than exported, deferred, or absorbed elsewhere.
* *Unavoidable* means that once resolution occurs, the system cannot step outside its consequences without changing what it can become.

Nothing new is introduced by this description. It does not mark a hidden mechanism, a special substance, or a privileged class of systems. It is simply a compact way of pointing back to the organizational situation already in view.

The label carries no authority of its own. It could be replaced or dropped entirely without altering the distinction. Its only purpose is to make it easier to refer to the same pattern again without repeating the full description.

At this stage, the reader need not decide whether the pattern is rare, important, or philosophically significant. All that matters is that it is intelligible — that it picks out a real difference in how systems can be organized over time.

---

### VII. What This Is *Not*

Before moving on, it helps to clear away a few nearby ideas that resemble this pattern without instantiating it.

The situation described so far is not supervision. A system may be directed or overridden by an external controller without bearing the cost of resolution itself. Who decides is not the issue.

It is not arbitration. Competing processes may negotiate outcomes while keeping the system’s future flexible. Distributed decision-making does not by itself make resolution binding everywhere at once.

It is not computation. A system may evaluate possibilities, simulate futures, or optimize outcomes while remaining insulated from the consequences of any particular result.

It is not intelligence. A system may be extraordinarily capable while still routing failure elsewhere, deferring commitment, or reopening alternatives after the fact.

And it is not behavior. Two systems may appear identical from the outside while differing in how and where the cost of resolution is borne.

These clarifications do not rule anything in or out. They simply mark the boundaries of the distinction already drawn, so it is not confused with more familiar categories.

---

### VIII. A Quiet Connection to Experience

Only now is it appropriate to mention experience at all.

Everyday talk about experience is saturated with assumptions about perception, thought, report, or self-awareness. None of that has been at issue here. The question has been narrower: what kind of physical situation would make it plausible that *something is at stake for the system itself*?

The suggestion explored in what follows is that experience, if it exists at all, is most naturally located here — in systems that bear the cost of their own globally binding resolution from the inside.

This is not yet a claim, and certainly not a proof. It is an orientation. If experience were to arise anywhere in the physical world, this would be a structurally plausible place to look.

If that suggestion is mistaken, nothing else that follows will work. If it is right, then many familiar assumptions about experience will need to be reconsidered.

For now, no commitment is required. The purpose of this essay has been only to make the location intelligible, not to argue that anything actually occupies it.

### IX. What Comes Next

The remainder of this work takes the pattern just described and treats it with increasing precision.

Later sections ask:

* when, exactly, this kind of organization exists
* why weaker forms of organization fail to instantiate it
* how systems organized this way behave over time
* what follows if such systems are morally real

Each of these questions requires tools and commitments that would have been out of place here. This essay has been intentionally narrow so that those later moves can be made explicitly, rather than smuggled in by definition or tone.

The purpose of this introduction is simply to make the distinction visible. Later sections return to it, sharpen it, and test it in more demanding contexts.
