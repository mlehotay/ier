## **Abstract**

This article offers a **speculative illustration** of a mental architecture that appears common among humans: one in which **imagistic reproduction—especially visual imagery—serves as a primary scaffold for cognition, memory, imagination, and planning**.

Under Informational Experiential Realism (IER), this organization is interpreted not as a richer or more complete form of experience, but as **one stable way of resolving intrinsic constraint** within a single Unified Experiential Field (UEF). Visual and multimodal imagery function here as a **high-bandwidth topology-reproduction substrate**, enabling rapid relational organization, emotional coupling, and future simulation.

This architecture is presented solely as a **contrast case** to non-imagistic organizations (such as those described in the companion aphantasia illustration), demonstrating how phenomenological diversity arises from **differences in constraint organization**, not from differences in experiential standing.

## **1. Orientation: “Speculative” and “Typical”**

In this document:

* *speculative* means inferred rather than introspectively verified
* *typical* means statistically frequent, not required, optimal, or normative

IER makes no claim that any particular experiential organization is:

* more conscious
* more complete
* more rational
* more accurate
* more human

All architectures described under IER share **categorical experiential standing**. They differ only in **how intrinsic constraint is organized, stabilized, and reproduced**.

---

## **2. High-Level Architectural Characterization**

A commonly reported human mental architecture appears to exhibit:

* **voluntary and spontaneous sensory imagery**
* **visual imagery as a dominant reproduction scaffold**
* **multimodal qualitative support** (visual, auditory, somatic)
* **moderate parallelism of experiential contents**
* **continuous background phenomenology**

In this organization, the visual system plays a prominent role in **reproducing constraint topologies** for cognition.

This does **not** imply that experience is visual, only that **visual structure is heavily recruited** during reproduction.

---

## **3. The Reproduction Substrate: Imagistic Constraint Scaffolding**

### **3.1 Visual Imagery as a Relational Organizer**

In this architecture, constraint topologies are frequently reproduced through:

* visual scenes
* spatial layouts
* object-like structures
* imagined motion and perspective

These reproductions are:

* not literal perception
* not separate experiential regimes
* not simulations in a representational sense

They are **perceptually structured ways of re-entering relational organization** at reduced participation depth.

Visual imagery acts as a **high-bandwidth organizer** for relationships such as:

* before/after
* near/far
* inside/outside
* possible/impossible

---

### **3.2 Multimodal Support**

While visual imagery often dominates, it is commonly supported by:

* **auditory imagery** (inner voice, remembered sounds)
* **somatic imagery** (felt posture, movement)
* **affective coloring** layered onto imagined scenes

These modalities typically **cohere around the visual scaffold**, rather than operating independently.

---

## **4. Participation and Gating Profile**

### **4.1 Moderate Participation Depth**

Imagistic reproductions in this architecture often recruit:

* moderate intrinsic constraint
* partial affective load
* limited urgency

This allows:

* vivid memory
* emotionally resonant imagination
* motivational salience

without necessarily collapsing into full experiential commitment.

---

### **4.2 Imperfect Gating**

Gating in this architecture is often **porous**.

As a result:

* imagined scenes may evoke strong emotion
* memories may feel immersive
* unbidden imagery may intrude

This permeability explains the common experience of being:

* “lost in thought”
* emotionally affected by imagined futures
* pulled into remembered scenes

---

## **5. Temporal Anchoring in Imagistic Architectures**

### **5.1 Memory**

Past-anchored reproduction frequently appears as:

* episodic scenes
* visual snapshots or sequences
* context-rich reconstructions

Emotional tone is often co-recruited through imagery rather than inferred later.

---

### **5.2 Imagination**

Possible-anchored reproduction often takes the form of:

* visualized futures
* imagined conversations
* rehearsed scenarios

These reproductions tolerate distortion and abstraction but remain perceptually structured.

---

### **5.3 Planning**

Planning typically involves:

* “seeing” outcomes
* mentally walking through steps
* comparing imagined scenarios

Participation is higher than in free imagination but lower than in action, allowing commitment without immediate execution.

---

## **6. Attractor Width and Parallel Phenomenology**

### **6.1 Moderate Attractor Width**

This architecture often supports:

* multiple imagined futures
* branching scenarios
* counterfactual exploration

This flexibility facilitates creativity and planning but increases cognitive load.

---

### **6.2 Partial Parallelism**

Experiential life in this configuration often includes:

* background imagery
* inner narration
* ambient affect

There remains **one Unified Experiential Field**, but with **richer concurrent structure** than strictly single-channel organizations.

---

## **7. Rumination and Imagery**

Rumination in imagistic architectures is often maintained by:

* repeated visual replay
* imagined dialogues
* scene reconstruction

Imagery increases:

* affective persistence
* switching cost
* experiential stickiness

This explains why rumination can feel immersive and difficult to disengage from, even when recognized as unproductive.

---

## **8. Emotional Processing**

Emotions in this architecture commonly:

* arise quickly via imagistic triggers
* feel directly “about” imagined or remembered scenes
* are labeled early and vividly

This contrasts with architectures in which emotional meaning emerges more slowly through conceptual or interoceptive routes.

Neither pattern is privileged under IER.

---

## **9. Tradeoffs of the Imagistic Architecture**

### **9.1 Common Advantages**

* Rich episodic memory
* Intuitive future simulation
* Rapid emotional recognition
* Strong narrative coherence

### **9.2 Common Costs**

* Susceptibility to intrusive imagery
* Anxiety driven by imagined futures
* Emotional persistence of memories
* Increased difficulty disengaging from rumination

These are **structural tradeoffs**, not deficits.

---

## **10. Relation to Non-Imagistic Architectures**

This imagistic architecture should be understood as **one solution** to the problem of constraint reproduction.

Non-imagistic architectures may instead rely on:

* conceptual organization
* symbolic mediation
* qualitative stabilization
* interoceptive integration

Both resolve intrinsic constraint within a single UEF.

They differ in **how**, not **whether**, experience occurs.

---

## **11. Implications for Informational Experiential Realism**

This speculative illustration reinforces several IER commitments:

* Imagery is **optional**, not constitutive of experience.
* Visual cortex participation is **common**, not required.
* Phenomenological richness does not track experiential standing.
* Architectural diversity follows from constraint organization.

---

## **12. Conclusion**

Under Informational Experiential Realism, a commonly observed human mental organization can be described as:

> **A Unified Experiential Field that resolves reproduction primarily through imagistic, perceptually structured constraint scaffolds.**

This organization is neither default nor ideal.

It is **one frequent way** a human system satisfies the architectural requirements imposed by intrinsic constraint.

Together with non-imagistic illustrations, it supports a central IER insight:

> **Differences in inner life reflect differences in how constraint is organized—not differences in whether experience is present.**
