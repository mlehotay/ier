# Constraint

## Constraint as Lawful Restriction on Admissible Continuation

**Informational Experiential Realism (IER v10.10.7)**  
*Tier 2 · Foundational Clarification · Canon-Constrained · Non-Normative*  

## Status, Scope, and Authority

This document is explanatory and non-normative.

It:

* introduces no new ontological primitives
* does not redefine intrinsic constraint
* introduces no probabilistic or informational measures
* introduces no causal or teleological mechanisms
* establishes no criteria, thresholds, or diagnostics

The *IER-specification* and the Tier-1 foundation layer retain ontological and structural authority.

This document is foundation-level but does not override the specification.

If any statement here conflicts with the specification, the specification prevails.

## Orientation

In many scientific and philosophical traditions the word *constraint* is interpreted as:

* a force that pushes systems
* a rule that selects outcomes
* an informational limitation
* a control mechanism
* an optimization pressure

These interpretations are incompatible with Informational Experiential Realism.

IER uses the term constraint in a strictly structural sense.

Constraint does not:

* act
* push
* evaluate
* choose
* select among alternatives.

Constraint only determines which transitions between configurations are physically admissible.

Constraint therefore defines the structure of possible continuation.

All frontier dynamics occur within that structure.

### Structural Clarifications

Within this document, two derived properties of constraint are used:

* *Slack* refers to the multiplicity of admissible futures available under a given constraint structure.
* *Saturation* refers to the reduction of that multiplicity due to increasing constraint.

These are not additional primitives. They are descriptive properties of admissible transition structure.

Multiplicity of admissible futures does not imply probability, likelihood, or selection.
It is a structural property only.

Information is not treated as content, message, or representation.
It is defined strictly as physically instantiated distinctions in admissible transition structure.

Admissible futures are the set of physically possible continuations permitted by the system’s constraint structure. This multiplicity is structural only and does not imply probability, uncertainty, or selection.

## Constraint in the Dynamical Schema

IER represents system dynamics using a minimal reachability structure.

Let $S$ be the configuration space of a system.

Let $R \subseteq S \times S$ be the admissible transition relation.

For a configuration $s \in S$, the admissible successor set is:

$$
A(s) = \{ s' \in S \mid (s,s') \in R \}
$$

The set $A(s)$ defines the admissible futures of the system at the history-future boundary.

Constraint appears in the structure of the relation $R$, which determines which transitions are permitted.

Constraint therefore specifies:

* which continuations are admissible
* which continuations are impossible
* how admissible futures are structured.

Constraint does not determine which admissible continuation occurs.

## Constraint and Admissible Futures

The admissible successor set $A(s)$ defines the future cone available at a frontier configuration.

Constraint determines the geometry of this cone.

Multiple admissible continuations may exist simultaneously.

Multiplicity arises because constraint permits more than one continuation that remains jointly sustainable under physical law.

IER therefore treats admissible futures as a structural object defined by constraint.

Frontier processes such as:

* bias
* collapse
* propagation

operate within the admissible continuation structure determined by constraint.

## Constraint and Causation

Constraint is not causation.

Causation concerns actual transitions between configurations.

Constraint concerns which transitions are physically admissible.

Constraint therefore:

* does not transmit energy
* does not produce motion
* does not generate events.

Constraint only restricts which transitions may occur.

Actual transitions remain governed by the lawful dynamics of the system.

## Constraint and Information

Constraint and information are structurally distinct.

Constraint concerns the lawful restriction on transitions.

Information concerns the exclusion of otherwise admissible continuations produced by organization under constraint, as defined in *IER-information*.

Constraint defines the space of admissible continuation.

Information arises when a system’s organization eliminates possibilities within that space.

The structural relation is therefore:

* constraint $\to$ admissible continuation
* organization $\to$ exclusion within that continuation
* information $\to$ resulting structured impossibility

Constraint exists independently of informational structure.

## Constraint and Intrinsic Closure

Constraint exists wherever physical law restricts continuation.

Intrinsic closure is a stronger condition.

As clarified in *IER-intrinsic-closure*, a regime is intrinsically closed when admissible futures do not factorize across subsystem partitions.

For subsystem components $s_A, s_B$, intrinsic closure is defined as follows:

### Intrinsic closure condition

$$
A(s) \neq A_A(s_A) \times A_B(s_B)
$$

In such regimes:

* subsystem continuations constrain one another
* admissible futures form a single integrated structure
* the regime cannot be decomposed into independent dynamics.

Constraint therefore permits closure but does not imply it.

Intrinsic closure describes global coupling of admissible futures under constraint.

## Constraint and Incompatibility

When intrinsic closure holds, subsystem continuations cannot vary independently.

Some combinations of subsystem continuations become jointly unsustainable.

This produces incompatibility.

Incompatibility occurs when:

> multiple admissible continuations cannot remain jointly sustainable under global constraint.

Constraint itself does not eliminate these alternatives.

However, because closure prevents exporting incompatibility outside the regime, some alternatives must eventually become unsustainable.

This condition gives rise to exclusion.

## Constraint and Collapse

Collapse is defined in *IER-collapse* as:

> the irreversible foreclosure of a connected region of admissible futures at the history-future boundary under intrinsic constraint.

Constraint structures the continuation space in which collapse occurs.

Constraint does not perform collapse.

Collapse is an event in which a region of admissible continuation becomes irreversibly unreachable.

Constraint therefore determines the geometry of possibility within which collapse may occur.

## Constraint and Frontier Geometry

Constraint shapes the geometry of admissible continuation at the frontier.

This geometry determines:

* reachability structure
* reachability margins
* switching cost structure
* viability regions

These structural properties influence:

* bias deformation
* collapse pathways
* propagation of structural change.

Constraint therefore determines the geometric structure of continuation within which frontier dynamics unfold.

## Constraint and Physical Law

IER does not redefine physical law.

Constraint refers simply to lawful restriction on physical continuation.

Constraint may arise from:

* conservation laws
* interaction structure
* boundary conditions
* coupling architecture
* material organization.

IER remains compatible with any lawful physical dynamics.

Constraint therefore functions as a general structural description of lawful restriction, not a separate mechanism.

## Blocked Misinterpretations

Within Informational Experiential Realism, constraint must not be interpreted as:

* a force acting on a system
* informational content
* optimization pressure
* a selector among alternatives
* a rule evaluating outcomes
* a computational program
* a semantic restriction.

Constraint introduces no agency, evaluation, or teleology.

Constraint is lawful restriction on admissible continuation.

Nothing more.

## Structural Summary

Constraint is:

* lawful
* structural
* non-agentive
* non-informational
* non-teleological.

Constraint determines which continuations are physically admissible.

Within the continuation structure defined by constraint:

* intrinsic closure may arise
* incompatibility may appear
* exclusion may occur
* collapse may irreversibly foreclose futures.

Constraint itself performs none of these operations.

Constraint defines the space of possible continuation within which frontier dynamics unfold.
