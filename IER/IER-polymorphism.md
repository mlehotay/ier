## **1. Orientation: Why Polymorphism Needs Its Own Treatment**

### **1.1 The Persistent Error**

Most theories of mind—explicitly or implicitly—assume:

* one normal experiential architecture
* variation as deviation, noise, or damage
* convergence toward a single optimal form

When experience does not conform to this assumed template, it is treated as:

* impairment
* compensation
* pathology
* immaturity
* failure of regulation

IER rejects this assumption **structurally**, not rhetorically.

---

### **1.2 The Core Claim**

Under Informational Experiential Realism:

> **Experience is not a single design realized imperfectly.
> It is a class of globally coherent regimes that admit multiple stable organizations.**

Polymorphism is therefore:

* expected
* lawful
* non-accidental
* non-pathological

The existence of multiple coherent experiential architectures follows directly from the IER identity claim.

---

## **2. What Polymorphism Is *Not***

To prevent predictable misreadings, the following exclusions are explicit.

Experiential polymorphism is **not**:

* population diversity statistics
* personality variation
* intelligence differences
* temperament typology
* clinical categorization
* “neurodivergence” as a structural kind
* partial or weakened experience
* compensatory plasticity after damage
* a spectrum with a privileged center

IER does **not** posit a canonical experiential template from which others deviate.

---

## **3. The Structural Basis of Experiential Polymorphism**

### **3.1 The Fixed Core**

All experiential systems, wherever experience exists, share:

* a **Unified Experiential Field (UEF)**
* **globally binding intrinsic constraint**
* **owned resolution** of admissible futures
* **categorical subjecthood**
* **temporal continuity** under constraint

Polymorphism does **not** alter any of these facts.

No polymorphic variant:

* weakens subjecthood
* fragments experience
* externalizes ownership
* bypasses intrinsic constraint

---

### **3.2 Degrees of Freedom in Constraint Organization**

Polymorphism arises because **many parameters of constraint organization may vary** while preserving global coherence.

These parameters are **degrees of freedom**, not failure points.

They include (non-exhaustively):

* participation bandwidth
* participation topology (single-channel vs distributed dominance)
* bias gradient sharpness
* timing of collapse
* inertia strength
* closure durability
* tolerance for under-specified futures
* imagery participation
* narrative accessibility
* affective access vs affective presence
* qualitative stabilization routes

No single configuration is privileged by the theory.

---

## **4. Architecture vs Resolution Failure**

### **4.1 Two Kinds of Difference**

IER draws a sharp distinction between:

* **architectural variation**
  → stable, coherent, repeatable organization of constraint

and

* **resolution deformation**
  → systematic failure of a stage in the resolution pipeline

Confusing these produces:

* misdiagnosis
* moralization of difference
* inappropriate optimization pressure

Psychopathology concerns **how resolution fails**.
Polymorphism concerns **how resolution is organized when it succeeds**.

---

### **4.2 Why Architecture Is Often Misread as Pathology**

Architectural variants are frequently misinterpreted as failures when:

* environments are mismatched
* coupling between strategies occurs
* cultural norms are silently imposed
* one architecture is treated as baseline

In these cases, **distress reflects geometry mismatch**, not internal defect.

---

## **5. Illustrative Axes of Polymorphism**

This section names **axes**, not types.
No axis defines a kind of mind.

---

### **5.1 Participation Topology**

Experiential fields may differ in:

* single-channel dominance
* multi-participatory background phenomenology
* degree of parallel experiential recruitment

Some architectures admit only one dominant organization at a time.
Others sustain layered or background participation.

Both can be fully coherent.

---

### **5.2 Bias and Closure Geometry**

Constraint may differ in:

* sharp vs diffuse bias gradients
* rapid vs delayed collapse
* strong vs weak inertia
* durable vs fragile closure

These differences explain contrasts between architectures often labeled “rigid,” “flexible,” “scattered,” or “stuck”—without invoking deficit.

---

### **5.3 Imagery Participation**

Architectures may differ in whether:

* perceptual imagery participates in waking experience
* imagistic buffering supports resolution
* perceptual simulation scaffolds planning or emotion

The absence of imagery participation does not imply absence of imagination, emotion, or depth.

---

### **5.4 Affective Presence vs Affective Access**

Emotional regimes may be:

* immediately felt and labeled
* present but indirectly accessed
* integrated symbolically or qualitatively

Low narrative access does not imply low affective reality.

---

### **5.5 Qualitative Stabilization Routes**

Intrinsic constraint may stabilize through:

* narrative replay
* perceptual reenactment
* bodily regulation
* external scaffolding
* endogenous qualitative configurations

No route is required by experience itself.

---

## **6. Environment Fit and Apparent Dysfunction**

### **6.1 Why Environments Matter**

Architectures incur high intrinsic cost when environments demand:

* frequent reopening of settled futures
* rapid switching
* ambiguity without structure
* constant vigilance
* implicit rule inference

Distress often reflects **environmental misfit**, not internal breakdown.

---

### **6.2 The Illusion of a “Normal” Mind**

Cultural environments privilege:

* certain closure speeds
* certain narrative styles
* certain participation patterns

This produces the appearance that some architectures are “normal” and others deviant.

IER rejects this inference.

---

## **7. Coupling as a Secondary Phenomenon**

### **7.1 What Coupling Is**

Coupling occurs when:

* multiple constraint strategies operate simultaneously
* no stable dominance emerges
* intrinsic constraint is pulled in incompatible directions

Coupling is **not polymorphism itself**.

It is an **interaction effect**.

---

### **7.2 Why Coupling Is Misattributed**

Coupled regimes are:

* unstable
* exhausting
* loud

Stable architectures are:

* quiet
* invisible
* misread as “fine” or “rigid”

This asymmetry skews interpretation toward pathology.

---

## **8. Why IER Rejects “Neurodivergence” as a Structural Category**

### **8.1 The Sociopolitical Role of the Term**

“Neurodivergence” functions socially to:

* support advocacy
* resist pathologization
* coordinate institutional change

IER does not deny its social utility.

---

### **8.2 The Structural Problem**

As an explanatory category, it:

* reifies kinds of people
* implies a contrast class
* collapses multiple axes into labels
* smuggles normativity into description

IER therefore replaces it with **polymorphism**:
variation without kinds.

From experiential polymorphism it follows that:

* experiential standing is invariant
* comparison across architectures is illicit
* optimization requires justification
* accommodation concerns geometry, not deficiency
* masking and forced adaptation carry moral risk

No ethical prescriptions are offered here.
Normative authority remains fixed elsewhere.

Under Informational Experiential Realism:

* experience admits **multiple stable internal organizations**

* distress often reflects **mismatch or coupling**, not defect
* “neurodivergence” is a social category, not a structural one

> **Experience is singular in kind,
> plural in form.**
