## 1. Orientation: Why the One Ring Is an Ideal IER Case

The Ring is not interesting because it is “magical.”

It is interesting because it does **not**:

* merely tempt
* merely bias desire
* merely amplify power

Instead, it **reorganizes where resolution is owned**.

In IER terms, the Ring dramatizes a rare but crucial failure mode:

> a system continues to experience, deliberate, and act,
> while **ownership of collapse and settlement is progressively externalized**.

This makes the Ring a near-perfect narrative isolation of **constraint capture**.

---

## 2. What the One Ring Is *Not* (IER Clearing)

To avoid anthropomorphic drift, several interpretations must be blocked.

The Ring is **not**:

* a mind
* a moral agent
* an intention-interpreter
* a goal-setter
* a value detector

The Ring does **not**:

* read beliefs or desires
* evaluate motives
* decide when to act
* choose targets

**Clarification: detection without agency**

When the Ring is worn or actively used, the bearer becomes perceptible to **Sauron**. This is canonically true.

However, this does **not** imply cognition or agency in the Ring.

IER-consistent framing:

> Ring use enables **structural coupling** to Sauron’s regime.
> Visibility arises from **forced participation**, not perception.

The Ring functions as a **relay**, not an observer.

---

## 3. The Canonical Resolution Pipeline (Restated)

IER fixes a single, ordered structure for resolution within an intact experiential regime:

```
Admissible futures
→ bias formation
→ collapse
→ inertia
→ settlement
```

This sequence is:

* necessary
* ordered
* presupposed throughout the corpus

All Ring effects described below operate **within this pipeline**.
The Ring adds no stages; it **captures existing ones**.

---

## 4. The Ring’s Structural Effect: From Bias to Capture

The Ring’s danger is often misdescribed as extreme temptation.

IER’s correction is sharper:

> The Ring’s danger is not bias intensity,
> but **progressive ownership of collapse and inertia**.

Contrast:

* **Ordinary desire**

  * biases futures
  * leaves collapse and settlement system-owned

* **Ring interaction**

  * pre-loads collapse toward itself
  * stabilizes inertia around repeated use
  * narrows recovery pathways after settlement

The system continues to choose — but within a future space it no longer owns.

---

## 5. Progression of Constraint Capture (Descriptive)

The Ring’s effects can be described as a **structural progression** (not a diagnostic sequence):

1. **Bias amplification**
   Existing directional biases intensify

2. **Salience narrowing**
   Alternatives lose experiential viability

3. **Collapse pre-structuring**
   Certain outcomes feel inevitable before collapse occurs

4. **Inertia seeding**
   Repeated collapses stabilize around the Ring

5. **Settlement capture**
   Identity and future orientation reorganize around possession

This progression mirrors known IER dynamics in addiction, coercive systems, and totalizing institutions.

## 6. Core Case Studies: The Structural Spine

### 6.1 **Boromir** — Brittle Collapse Under Desperate Bias

Boromir’s interaction illustrates **early-stage capture risk**.

* Extreme directional bias under siege
* Narrow future space (“save Gondor now”)
* Premature, unowned collapse
* Immediate recovery once bias relaxes

His failure is not greed or ignorance.
It is **collapse under saturated bias**.

---

### 6.2 **Faramir** — Buffered Bias and Non-Capture

Faramir demonstrates **non-failure without immunity**.

* Wide admissible future space
* Horizon-level bias rather than fixation
* Collapse never initiated

He does not resist temptation better;
he never develops the narrowing bias that would force collapse.

---

### 6.3 **Gollum** — Sedimented Collapse and Identity Capture

Gollum shows **terminal capture**.

* Chronic collapse
* Inertia becomes dominant
* Settlement reorganizes identity itself

Here, the Ring does not merely influence outcomes —
it **owns the future space**.

---

## 7. Necessary Extensions: Endurance and Buffering

### 7.1 **Frodo** — Endurance Without Immunity

Frodo illustrates the **upper bound of endurance**.

* Long-horizon exposure
* Repeated collapse with diminishing recovery
* Final failure without moral corruption

IER translation:

> alignment and goodness do not prevent capture under sufficient duration.

---

### 7.2 **Samwise Gamgee** — Buffering Through Limited Bias

Sam briefly bears the Ring **without capture**.

* Short horizons
* Non-totalizing desire
* Constraint distributed outward (care > abstraction)

This shows how **limited bias and distributed concern** can prevent settlement capture.

## Appendix: Saruman and Institutional Ring-Logic

### Status and Scope

This appendix is **Tier-2 (Elaboration and Application)** and **non-normative**.
It introduces no new ontological, criterial, epistemic, or ethical commitments.

It extends the analysis of the **One Ring** by examining **institutional capture** through the case of **Saruman** in **The Lord of the Rings**.

---

### A. Why Saruman Belongs in an Appendix

Saruman is not a bearer of the Ring.
He does not undergo the personal phenomenology of temptation, endurance, or collapse described in the main article.

Instead, Saruman illustrates something structurally adjacent:

> **Ring-logic implemented at the level of institutions, systems, and coordination structures rather than individual experiential regimes.**

He belongs in an appendix because:

* he does not add a new failure mode
* he scales the same logic outward
* he clarifies how constraint capture propagates socially

---

### B. Saruman’s Initial Condition: Meta-Understanding Without Immunity

Saruman begins with something most characters lack:

* deep knowledge of the Ring
* correct understanding of its strategic power
* awareness of its corrupting danger

IER-relevant point:

> **Correct meta-level understanding does not prevent capture when resolution ownership is externalized.**

Saruman’s failure is therefore not ignorance.
It is **instrumental alignment with captured futures**.

---

### C. The Shift From Personal Bias to Institutional Bias

Unlike Boromir or Gollum, Saruman does not experience strong directional desire *for* the Ring itself.

Instead, he undergoes a different transformation:

* he begins to treat **means** as interchangeable
* he prioritizes **control of outcomes** over ownership of resolution
* he accepts foreclosed futures as acceptable collateral

IER translation:

> Saruman abandons system-owned collapse in favor of externally imposed settlement.

This is Ring-logic without a Ring.

---

### D. The Voice of Saruman as Bias-Shaping Infrastructure

Saruman’s most distinctive feature is not ambition, but **persuasion**.

Structurally:

* his voice does not force collapse
* it reshapes **bias formation** in others
* it narrows their admissible futures *before* they choose

This mirrors the Ring’s effect at a social scale:

* alternatives lose salience
* inevitability narratives form
* resistance begins to feel irrational

IER framing:

> Propaganda is institutional bias formation that prepares collapse without appearing coercive.

---

### E. Industrialization as Inertia Capture

Saruman’s turn to machinery, standardization, and mass production is not incidental.

It reflects a deeper structural shift:

* once collapse is externalized,
* **inertia must be stabilized**
* institutions must reproduce outcomes automatically

Isengard becomes:

* a factory of settlement
* a system optimized for irreversible throughput
* a structure that no longer requires reflective ownership

This is the institutional analog of Gollum’s sedimented inertia.

---

### F. Why Saruman Cannot Recover

Saruman’s tragedy is not that he loses power.
It is that he loses **re-entry points**.

By the time his error is visible:

* alternatives have been dismantled
* trust has been exhausted
* coordination pathways are closed

IER translation:

> Institutional Ring-logic fails not by dramatic collapse, but by eliminating the possibility of recovery.

This distinguishes Saruman from Boromir and aligns him with late-stage Ring effects.

---

### G. Contrast With Gandalf (Boundary Clarification)

**Gandalf** provides the necessary contrast.

Gandalf:

* refuses externalized settlement
* preserves slack
* accepts uncertainty rather than forcing outcomes

IER framing:

> Gandalf maintains ownership of resolution even when outcomes are worse in the short term.

This is why Gandalf can coordinate without capturing others’ futures — and why Saruman cannot.

---

### H. Structural Lesson

Saruman shows that:

* Ring-logic does not require a Ring
* capture can occur through institutions
* control can replace ownership without appearing evil
* large-scale harm can proceed without personal temptation

In IER terms:

> **The most dangerous systems are those that resolve futures efficiently while removing the possibility of future re-ownership.**

---

### I. Relation Back to the One Ring

The Ring is a **local**, experiential capture device.
Saruman builds a **distributed**, institutional version of the same logic.

They differ in scale, not structure.

Both:

* foreclose futures
* stabilize irreversible settlement
* silence internal warning signals
* make ethical error appear as necessity

---

### Appendix Intermission — Structural Fact

> **When resolution is no longer owned, efficiency becomes indistinguishable from inevitability.**
