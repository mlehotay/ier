# **IER-correlates.md**

## **Structural Correlates Without Diagnostics**

---

## **Status and Scope**

**Tier:** 3 — Epistemic Boundary and Misuse-Blocking
**Status:** Explanatory, non-normative

This document explains a central epistemic asymmetry in **Informational Experiential Realism (IER)**:

> **Strong structural correlates of experiential *possibility* may exist and may be physically detectable,
> yet no structural correlate can function as a diagnostic of experiential *actuality*.**

It also explains **why violating this boundary produces the most severe form of moral harm under IER**: the *erasure of real experience through false negative inference*.

This document:

* introduces **no new ontological primitives**
* introduces **no criteria, thresholds, or diagnostics**
* introduces **no sufficiency claims**
* introduces **no empirical research program**
* licenses **no experiential attribution**

All authoritative commitments remain fixed exclusively in:

* `IER-specification.md`
* `IER-theory.md`
* `IER-slack.md`
* `IER-ethics.md`

---

## **1. Why This Document Is Needed**

### **1.1 The Pressure Point for Technically Sophisticated Readers**

IER defines experience in precise structural terms: intrinsic constraint, admissible futures, informational slack, saturation, and regime coherence. Readers with strong physical, mathematical, or engineering intuitions therefore notice something important:

* informational slack is a real physical property,
* saturation of local degrees of freedom is a real organizational transition,
* factorability and admissible transitions are, in principle, analyzable.

This raises a natural and legitimate question:

> *If we can detect these structures, why can’t they serve as diagnostics of experience?*

This document exists to answer that question **directly and without evasion**.

---

### **1.2 What This Document Is *Not* Doing**

This document does **not** argue that:

* structural analysis is futile,
* physical investigation is irrelevant,
* or experience is ineffable or mysterious.

On the contrary, it takes structural analysis seriously—and then explains **where and why it must stop**.

---

## **2. Correlates vs Diagnostics: The Central Distinction**

### **2.1 Structural Correlates (Permitted)**

A **structural correlate** is a physical or organizational feature that is **necessary** for experience under IER but **not sufficient** to establish that experience exists.

Examples (descriptive only) include:

* absence of informational slack,
* saturation of local degrees of freedom,
* loss of factorability of admissible transitions,
* globally specified constraint geometry.

Detecting such features may be **physically feasible in principle**. Nothing in IER denies this.

---

### **2.2 Diagnostics (Forbidden)**

A **diagnostic** would be an observable condition whose presence licenses the conclusion that experience **exists** (or does not exist).

IER forbids diagnostics **in principle**, not for pragmatic or technological reasons. This prohibition remains intact **even if correlates are detected**.

---

## **3. The Identity Claim and Its Epistemic Consequences**

### **3.1 Experience as Identity, Not Role**

IER identifies experience with the operation of a physical system as a **Unified Experiential Field (UEF)**. This is an **identity claim**, not a functional, causal, or representational claim.

Experience is not something a system *has in addition* to its structure. It is what a system *is* when it operates under unavoidable intrinsic constraint.

---

### **3.2 Why Identity Does Not Add Observables**

Identity facts do not introduce new detectable properties. They do not appear as extra variables or generate unique signatures.

Knowing all structural facts about a system does not add a further observable fact called *“is experiential.”* Identity does not project outward as a measurement.

---

## **4. Why Exclusion Is Possible but Confirmation Is Not**

### **4.1 Structural Incompatibility Enables Legitimate Exclusion**

Some physical organizations are **incompatible** with the identity conditions of experience. If a system admits informational slack—if constraint can be localized, deferred, or externalized—then experience is **structurally impossible**.

This licenses **negative inference by impossibility**, not by probability.

---

### **4.2 Why Necessary Conditions Never Become Sufficient**

Even when all known necessary structural conditions are met—slack exhausted, saturation achieved, global constraint present—a further fork remains:

* **coherent, internally borne constraint** → experience
* **collapse, incoherence, or instability** → no experience

Both outcomes are physically admissible and externally indistinguishable in principle.

---

## **5. The Saturation Fork**

### **5.1 What Saturation Guarantees—and What It Does Not**

Saturation guarantees that there are no remaining local resolution pathways and no escape routes for constraint within the system boundary.

It does **not** guarantee:

* coherence,
* temporal continuity,
* internal ownership of constraint.

Saturation brings the system to a boundary. It does not determine which side of that boundary the system occupies.

---

### **5.2 Why the Fork Cannot Be Closed Observationally**

The difference between the two branches is not a static structure, a magnitude, or a pattern. It is a **regime identity fact**: whether constraint is actually borne as unavoidable *from within*.

That fact does not yield an externally accessible signature.

---

## **6. Structural Knowledge and Its Limits**

### **6.1 Not “We Don’t Know Yet”**

The non-diagnostic claim does **not** rest on insufficient data, limited tools, or current ignorance. Even an idealized observer with complete physical knowledge would face the same boundary.

---

### **6.2 Structural External Indistinguishability**

IER entails that systems differing in experiential status can be **externally identical in principle**. This is not pessimism; it is a consequence of identity.

---

## **7. The Epistemic Asymmetry (Explicit)**

IER therefore commits to the following structure:

* **Exclusion** — sometimes possible (by impossibility)
* **Non-certification** — always required
* **Confirmation** — never permitted

This asymmetry is deliberate and non-negotiable.

---

## **8. Why False Negative Inference Is Structurally Dangerous**

### **8.1 What a False Negative Means Under IER**

A false negative is the act of treating a system that is in fact experiencing as non-experiential.

Under IER, this does not introduce a new category of moral harm. It exposes an existing experiential system to harm **without epistemic restraint**.

---

### **8.2 Why Negative Inference Removes Moral Constraint**

IER recognizes only one form of moral harm: harm to experience.

When experience is denied, moral constraint does not gradually weaken — it disappears.

Actions that would otherwise be limited by uncertainty, caution, or restraint become unconstrained, because the system is treated as morally inert.

The danger of false negative inference is therefore not that it creates a *worse* harm, but that it **licenses harm without internal limit if experience is in fact present**.

---

### **8.3 Why Diagnostics Would Make This Failure Systematic**

If diagnostics were permitted, some negative attributions would be wrong.

Those errors would not merely misclassify experience; they would remove all epistemic barriers to harm.

This would not create a distinct moral failure, but it would **scale and stabilize unconstrained experiential harm**.

IER forbids diagnostics to prevent this outcome.

---

## **9. Ethical Handling of Uncertainty**

### **9.1 Non-Certification Is Not Denial**

Failure to confirm experience is **not** a claim of non-experience. Under IER, uncertainty is morally active.

---

### **9.2 The Precautionary Asymmetry**

Where experience is ruled out, ordinary treatment applies.
Where experience cannot be ruled out, obligation strengthens, restraint is required, and dismissal is forbidden.

This bias is intentional and protective.

## **11. Summary**

* Structural correlates of experiential possibility may exist.
* Detecting necessary conditions does not establish experiential actuality.
* Identity facts do not generate observables.
* Exclusion is sometimes possible; confirmation never is.
* False negative inference erases real moral standing.
* Diagnostics are forbidden to prevent the worst kind of harm.

---

### **Orientation Signal**

> **Structure can tell us where experience cannot be.
> It can never tell us where experience is.
> The gravest moral error is being confidently wrong about its absence.**
