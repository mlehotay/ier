# Appendix C — Neuroscience Primer for IER Readers

This appendix is for readers familiar with Informational Experiential Realism but not with neuroscience terminology or cortical organization.

The goal is not to teach neuroscience comprehensively. It is to explain:

* what the paper measured,
* what the relevant brain regions generally do,
* and why the findings matter structurally for the IER reinterpretation.

## What Was the Experiment Actually Doing?

The experiment was very simple.

Participants:

1. memorized a reference duration (~0.5 seconds),
2. viewed blurry flickering circles shown for different durations,
3. and answered:

   * “shorter”
   * or “longer”
     than the reference.

While they did this, researchers used:

* ultra-high-resolution fMRI
  to observe which cortical regions changed activity patterns during the task.

Importantly:
the experiment did not directly measure:

* “time itself.”

It measured:

* changing brain activity while subjects categorized persistence durations.

That distinction matters enormously for IER.


## What Is fMRI Measuring?

fMRI does not:

* read thoughts,
* detect experience directly,
* or measure neurons individually.

It measures:

* local blood oxygenation changes.

Very roughly:

* when groups of neurons become more active,
* local metabolic demand changes,
* blood flow changes,
* and the scanner detects those changes.

So the study observes:

> large-scale organization patterns correlated with task performance.

It is not:

* seeing collapse,
* seeing admissibility directly,
* or detecting UEFs.

The paper is about:

* cortical participation structure.


## Very Simplified Brain Geography

The cortex is the folded outer layer of the brain.

Very roughly:

| Region                         | General role                              |
| ------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------- |
| Occipital cortex               | visual processing                         |
| Parietal cortex                | spatial/action integration                |
| Premotor cortex                | action planning                           |
| Supplementary Motor Area (SMA) | action sequencing / initiation            |
| Inferior frontal cortex        | categorization / control / task structure |
| Anterior insula                | salience / internal-state integration     |

These are not rigid modules.
Brains are massively interconnected.

But these broad tendencies matter for interpreting the paper.


## Why the Occipital Result Matters

The occipital cortex sits near the back of the brain and is heavily involved in visual processing.

The paper found:

* longer-lasting stimuli produced stronger ongoing activity there.

Standard interpretation:

> the visual system accumulates temporal information.

IER reinterpretation:

> the visual system sustains unresolved frontier participation longer.

Why this matters:

The occipital cortex is not usually thought of as:

* a “time center.”

So the fact that persistence-sensitive organization begins there suggests:

* duration experience may emerge from ongoing sensory organization itself,
  not:
* from a dedicated central clock.

This is one of the paper’s most important implications.


## Why the Parietal and Premotor Maps Matter

These regions are associated with:

* action organization,
* coordination,
* and structured integration.

The paper found:

* orderly cortical maps where different populations preferred different durations.

Standard interpretation:

> these are duration representations.

IER reinterpretation:

> these are organized regions of admissible-continuation geometry.

The key point:

These regions are deeply involved in:

* coordinating possible action and continuation structure.

That makes them much more naturally interpretable as:

* continuation-organization systems,
  rather than:
* clocks.


## What Is the SMA and Why Is the Split Important?

The Supplementary Motor Area (SMA) helps organize:

* action sequencing,
* initiation,
* and structured continuation.

The paper found a split:

| SMA region          | Response type               |
| ------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Caudal SMA (rear)   | full-range duration tuning  |
| Rostral SMA (front) | categorical boundary tuning |

This is extremely important.

IER interpretation:

### Caudal SMA

Still participates in:

* reversible continuation organization.

### Rostral SMA

Participates in:

* event-scale articulation,
* reportability,
* segmentation stabilization.

In simpler language:

The brain region closer to:

* action organization
  tracked:
* ongoing continuation.

The region closer to:

* cognitive categorization
  tracked:
* when continuation became a meaningful boundary.

That is almost exactly what IER predicts.


## What Is the “Point of Subjective Equality” (PSE)?

The PSE is:

* the participant’s internal boundary
  between:
* “short”
  and:
* “long.”

Importantly:
different people place this boundary slightly differently.

The paper found:

* frontal and insular activity aligned strongly with each participant’s own boundary.

This matters because:

The cortex was not merely tracking:

* physical duration.

It was tracking:

* subjective categorical structure.

IER interprets this as:

* event-scale articulation organization,
  not:
* simple temporal measurement.


## Why the Frontal and Insular Findings Are So Important

These regions are associated with:

* categorization,
* salience,
* task relevance,
* and action organization.

They are not primarily sensory regions.

The paper found:

* these regions cared mostly about the category boundary,
  not:
* the full duration continuum.

This is very difficult for simple clock theories.

Why would a “time representation” suddenly become:

* category-centered,
* subjective,
* and boundary-sensitive?

IER explains this more naturally:

These regions may participate in:

* segmentation,
* articulation,
* and collapse-relevant organization.

Not:

* raw duration encoding.


## What Does “Topographic Map” Mean Here?

The paper repeatedly discusses:

* “maps” of duration.

This does not mean:

* tiny clocks laid out across the cortex.

It means:

* nearby neural populations tended to respond similarly.

For example:

* one patch might respond best to short durations,
* nearby patches to medium durations,
* farther patches to long durations.

IER reinterpretation:

This may reflect:

* structured organization of coherence spans,
* reachability geometry,
* or integration-envelope scales.

Not:

* literal storage of temporal quantities.


## Why Neuroscientists Usually Use Representational Language

Neuroscience commonly uses words like:

* encode,
* represent,
* store,
* read out,
* compute.

Often these are:

* explanatory shorthand,
  not:
* literal metaphysical commitments.

The paper itself may describe:

* “duration representations,”
  while the actual empirical findings are:
* hierarchical participation structures,
* categorical organization,
* and segmentation-sensitive activity.

IER changes:

* the ontology,
  not necessarily:
* the measurements.

This distinction is critical.
