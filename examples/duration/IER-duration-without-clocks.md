# Duration Without Clocks

## Cortical Timing as Frontier Geometry

### An IER Reinterpretation of Visual Duration Tuning as Organization of Admissible Futures

## Abstract

A recent 7T fMRI study by Centanino, Fortunato, and Bueti identified a distributed cortical hierarchy associated with visual duration categorization. Early visual regions exhibited monotonic persistence-sensitive responses, parietal and premotor regions displayed topographically organized duration tuning across the presented interval range, and frontal — insular regions preferentially encoded subjective category boundaries associated with participants’ points of subjective equality (PSEs).

The original interpretation frames these findings as evidence for distributed neural representation of subsecond duration. This article proposes an alternative interpretation grounded in Informational Experiential Realism (IER). Under IER, subjective time does not arise from internal clocks, stored temporal variables, or traversal through an internal timeline. Instead, temporal phenomenology emerges from the organization of admissible continuation under intrinsic constraint: reversible frontier organization, reachability geometry, collapse-adjacent segmentation, welding, and propagation across nested integration envelopes.

From this perspective, the cortical hierarchy identified in the study may not primarily encode duration itself. Rather, it may organize admissible-continuation topology and event-scale articulation within a frontier-resolution architecture. The resulting framework interprets temporal phenomenology not as measurement of elapsed time, but as the experiential projection of organized admissible futures under irreversible constraint dynamics.

## What the Study Found

Participants in the experiment were trained to internalize a half-second reference interval. During scanning, they viewed blurry flickering circles presented for durations ranging from 0.2 to 0.8 seconds and categorized each stimulus as either:

* shorter,
  or:
* longer
  than the memorized reference.

The resulting cortical hierarchy displayed three major organizational regimes.


### Early Visual Cortex

Occipital visual regions displayed:

* monotonic persistence-sensitive responses,
* with stronger activity for longer stimulus durations.

The authors interpret these responses as sensory accumulation related to duration encoding.

Importantly, these regions did not display full-range categorical tuning.

Their responses tracked:

* ongoing persistence.


### Parietal, Premotor, and Caudal SMA Regions

Intermediate cortical regions displayed:

* unimodal tuning across the full duration range,
* organized topographically across the cortex.

Different neuronal populations preferentially responded to:

* short,
* medium,
* or long durations.

These maps appeared in:

* intraparietal sulcus,
* inferior parietal cortex,
* premotor cortex,
* and caudal supplementary motor area (SMA).

The authors interpret these structures as distributed duration-readout systems.


### Rostral SMA, Inferior Frontal Cortex, and Anterior Insula

Higher-order frontal regions displayed a dramatically different profile.

Rather than encoding the full duration continuum, these regions preferentially responded near:

* the participant’s subjective categorization boundary,
* or Point of Subjective Equality (PSE).

The tuning was therefore:

* categorical,
* subjective,
* and task-relative.

This is among the study’s most important findings.


### Hierarchical Clustering

The authors additionally identified:

* sensory,
* cognitive,
* and motor-oriented cortical clusters,
  organized hierarchically.

The resulting system does not resemble:

* a centralized timing mechanism.

Instead, it resembles:

* a layered transformation hierarchy.
