## One Hose, Changing Boundary

A garden hose runs.

Water flows outward in a continuous stream.

Now place your thumb partly over the opening.

The stream changes shape.

Adjust the angle.

It changes again.

Nothing about the water’s substance changes.

What changes is the boundary condition at the outlet.

Small differences in constraint at the boundary produce distinct, stable flow patterns.


## Regimes: Stable Flow Patterns

With no thumb:

* the stream is wide.

With moderate pressure:

* the stream becomes narrow and concentrated.

With a different angle:

* the stream may fan outward.

Each of these is:

* stable under the current boundary condition
* sustained across time
* distinct in organization

They are not momentary flickers.

They are stable patterns of flow under a given constraint configuration.

In *IER-taxonomy*, this corresponds to a regime:

> a temporally extended, globally coherent organization that persists and shapes what can happen next.

The hose remains the same hose.

The flow pattern changes with the boundary.


## Transitions: Reorganization Moments

Now move your thumb quickly from one position to another.

For a brief interval:

* the stream loses its old shape
* reorganizes
* settles into a new stable pattern

That brief interval is not itself a stable pattern.

It is the reorganization event between patterns.

In the taxonomy, this corresponds to a transition:

> a temporally compact reorganization that establishes or hands off between regimes.

The unstable moment is short.

The new pattern persists.


## Excursions: Fluctuations Within a Pattern

Even when your thumb remains steady, the stream may:

* flicker slightly
* ripple
* wobble
* show brief irregularities

Then it returns to the same overall pattern.

The boundary condition has not changed.

The organization has not reorganized.

These are temporary deviations.

In *IER-taxonomy*, this corresponds to an excursion:

> a transient deviation within a regime that resolves without altering global organization.

The stream never left its pattern.

It fluctuated and returned.


## State Evolution: Gradual Change Without Reorganization

Now imagine adjusting your thumb very slightly — not enough to create a visibly different kind of stream, but enough to alter:

* how tightly the stream holds together
* how far it travels
* how consistently it maintains its form

The overall pattern remains recognizable.

It is still the “narrow jet” or still the “wide stream.”

But its quantitative characteristics shift.

Nothing reorganizes into a new kind.

The pattern persists, modified.

*IER-state-evolution* names this:

> lawful, within-regime reconfiguration without changing the regime’s identity.

The hose metaphor captures this as:

* same pattern category
* altered parameters within that category

No new regime appears.

No transition occurs.

The existing organization continues in a slightly different configuration.


## Holding the Taxonomy with the Hose

The hose metaphor provides a way to visualize the structural limits laid out in *IER-taxonomy*.

At any moment, the stream is:

* in one stable pattern (regime),
* briefly reorganizing between patterns (transition), or
* temporarily fluctuating within a pattern (excursion).

And sometimes:

* the same pattern persists while its internal parameters shift (state evolution).

The metaphor does not add new categories.

It illustrates how many apparent differences reduce to:

* boundary configuration
* pattern stability
* reorganization
* fluctuation

All within a single continuous flow.


## Closing Image

A hose runs.

You adjust your thumb.

The stream widens.
It narrows.
It reorganizes.
It flickers.
It stabilizes again.

The water remains water.

The system remains one hose.

Different boundary configurations yield distinct stable patterns, brief reorganizations, and temporary fluctuations.

That is enough to hold:

* regimes
* transitions
* excursions
* state evolution

without multiplying kinds of events.


> One flow.
> Changing boundary conditions.
> Stable patterns.
> Brief reorganizations.
> Parameter shifts within the same pattern.

Use the hose to see the structure.

Then return to the taxonomy.
