Lena’s fingers clenched into fists. “Tired of feeling like this. Tired of waking up and hoping today won’t feel as empty as the last. Tired of—” She stopped, her voice cracking.  

Dr. Patel waited.  

“Tired of loving someone who doesn’t love me back.”  

The confession sat heavy between them. Lena glanced away, ashamed. She wasn’t supposed to care this much. It was just a crush—just feelings. But it didn’t feel small. It felt like an avalanche had buried her alive.  

“Tell me about them,” Dr. Patel said gently.  

Lena hesitated, then whispered, “Her name is Riley.”  

Dr. Patel said nothing, just nodded for her to continue.  

“She’s... everything.” Lena let out a hollow laugh. “She lights up every room she walks into. She’s got this laugh—God, you hear it, and it’s like the world makes sense for a second. She’s kind. She remembers the little things about people. And when she looks at you, it’s like she actually sees you.”  

Dr. Patel tilted her head. “But?”  

Lena clenched her jaw. “But she doesn’t look at me the way I look at her.”  

Silence settled over the office.  

“She talks about boys she likes,” Lena continued, voice raw. “And I sit there, listening, pretending it doesn’t hurt. Pretending I’m not breaking apart every time she gushes about someone else.”  

Dr. Patel’s expression was warm, steady. “That must be painful.”  

Lena let out a shaky breath. “Yeah.”  

“Have you told her how you feel?”  

Lena scoffed. “And ruin everything? She’s my best friend. If she knew, she’d probably just feel bad for me.” She looked down. “I’d rather keep my distance than have her pity.”  

Dr. Patel tapped her pen against her notepad. “So you’re grieving something you never really had.”  

Lena looked up, startled.  

Dr. Patel continued. “You’re mourning the idea of her loving you back. The version of reality you imagined, where she might have been yours.”  

Lena swallowed hard. “I guess.”  

“And grief is heavy,” Dr. Patel said. “Even for something that never happened. But you still deserve to heal from it.”  

Lena blinked rapidly, her vision blurring. “How do you heal from something like this?”  

Dr. Patel’s voice was gentle. “By letting yourself feel the loss. By acknowledging it was real to you, that your love was real. And by understanding that unrequited love doesn’t mean you’re unlovable.”

“Because your heart is tender,” Dr. Patel said. “And that’s not a bad thing.”  

Lena let out a breath she didn’t realize she was holding. The pain was still there, still sharp—but maybe, just maybe, it wouldn’t last forever.
