### **The Ghost in Room 7**  

The fluorescent lights buzzed overhead, casting a pale glow over the linoleum floor of St. Augustine Psychiatric Hospital. The halls smelled of antiseptic and something stale, like forgotten time.  

In Room 7, Lila sat on the bed, her long, bleached blonde hair falling over her face in tangled waves. Her hospital gown was too big, hanging off her thin frame, her arms wrapped tightly around her knees. She wasn’t sure how long she had been there—hours? Days? Time blurred under the weight of sedation and scrutiny.  

The last thing she remembered clearly was the motel room. The sound of sirens. The flashing red-and-blue lights. The way her own screams had sounded foreign to her, as if they had belonged to someone else.  

Now, she was on a 5150—an involuntary psychiatric hold. A nurse had explained it to her in a soft voice, like she was something fragile. “Seventy-two hours,” they had said. “For your safety.”  

But Lila wasn’t sure if safety existed anymore.  

A doctor had visited that morning, asking questions she didn’t want to answer. *Did you want to hurt yourself?* *Do you hear voices?* *Do you know where you are?* She had stared past him, watching the way the shadows moved along the walls, shifting even when nothing else did.  

At night, the other patients whispered. Some cried. The woman in the next room, an older lady with deep-set eyes, pressed her face against the glass window of her door and muttered about ghosts.  

Lila understood ghosts.  

Hers followed her everywhere.  

One had been in the motel room with her, just before the police came. She had felt its breath against her neck, had seen it in the mirror—her own reflection, but wrong. Her eyes had been too dark, her mouth stretching into a grin she hadn’t made. The ghost had spoken in her voice. *You don’t belong here.*  

Lila had believed it.  

Now, in Room 7, the ghost remained.  

It lurked in the corners, watching. Whispering things only she could hear. Telling her secrets no one else would believe.  

A nurse named David checked on her every few hours. He was kind, bringing her extra blankets even when she didn’t ask. “You’re gonna be okay, Lila,” he told her one night. “You’ll get out of here soon.”  

But she wasn’t sure if she *wanted* to leave.  

Because if she did, the ghost would follow.  

And next time, she might not be able to stop it.

The ghost had been with her for weeks, maybe months. It lived in her reflection, in the spaces between waking and sleeping. At first, it had only whispered. Then it started speaking in her voice, saying the things she tried to keep buried.  

*They don’t care about you.*  
*You are nothing.*  
*You should just disappear.*  

That night in the motel room, Lila had been exhausted—physically, mentally, in ways she didn’t have words for. She had checked into the room with shaky hands, using the last of her cash. The neon motel sign outside flickered erratically, casting flashes of red against the thin curtains.  

She had sat on the edge of the bed, staring at herself in the mirror above the dresser. That’s when the ghost spoke.  

*"You don’t belong here."*  

It wasn’t just a whisper this time. It was a command.  

Lila felt her body go cold. She knew what the ghost wanted. It wanted her to finish what she had started weeks ago, before the hospital, before the endless cycle of doctors and therapists and half-hearted promises.  

She stood and walked closer to the mirror. Her reflection wasn’t quite right. The eyes were too hollow. The mouth curled at the edges, amused.  

*"You know what to do."*  

Her hands trembled. There was a bottle of pills on the nightstand. A razor in her bag. It would be easy.  

But something inside her snapped.  

“No,” she whispered.  

The ghost’s expression shifted. The grin faltered.  

Lila clenched her fists. “No,” she said again, louder this time. “You don’t control me.”  

The ghost’s face twisted in rage. The lights flickered. A deep, suffocating pressure filled the room, like gravity had doubled.  

*"You can’t escape me."*  

Lila’s breath came in gasps, but she didn’t look away. “You’re not real,” she said. “You’re just… a part of me. And I don’t have to listen to you.”  

The ghost’s image flickered. The edges of its form blurred, distorted, unraveling.  

Then—just like that—it was gone.  

Lila had collapsed onto the bed, shaking. The room felt lighter. The air, breathable again.  

That was when the sirens came. The pounding on the door. The voices outside. Someone had called 911.  

And now, she was here. In St. Augustine.  

The ghost hadn’t spoken since that night. But Lila knew it was still watching. Waiting.  

She just wasn’t sure if she had defeated it… or if it was only biding its time.
