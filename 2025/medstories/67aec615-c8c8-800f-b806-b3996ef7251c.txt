“It sounds like you’re struggling with some really intense thoughts about yourself and your situation, Maya. These kinds of thoughts—where we see everything as extreme or all-or-nothing—are actually a form of what we call ‘cognitive distortions.’ They can make everything feel much worse than it actually is.”

Maya frowned. “Cognitive distortions? What’s that?”

“Cognitive distortions are basically patterns of thinking that can make situations seem worse, or even impossible, to handle,” Dr. Peterson explained gently. “For example, you mentioned that you feel like everything’s falling apart or that you always fail—those are examples of ‘catastrophizing,’ where you expect the worst outcome possible. Another common one is ‘all-or-nothing thinking,’ where you see things in black-and-white terms, like thinking that something is either a total success or a complete failure.”

Maya’s eyes widened. “That... sounds like me. I always think I’m failing or that I’ll never get it right.”

Dr. Peterson nodded. “You’re not alone in that. Many people struggle with these kinds of thoughts, and they can make it really hard to feel at peace or to see things realistically. But there’s good news. There’s a treatment called Dialectical Behavior Therapy, or DBT, that can help you recognize and challenge these distortions.”

“DBT?” Maya repeated, intrigued but uncertain. “What’s that?”

“It’s a type of therapy designed to help people manage emotions, cope with distress, and develop healthier ways of thinking and behaving. One of the main goals is to help you recognize cognitive distortions and learn how to reframe them. DBT also teaches skills for mindfulness, emotional regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness. It’s a practical approach that helps people feel more in control of their emotions and their thoughts.”

Maya felt a flicker of hope, though it was small. “Do you think... it could help me? I mean, I’ve tried other things before, but nothing seems to really work.”

“I understand that it might feel that way,” Dr. Peterson said, her tone compassionate. “DBT can be especially helpful when it comes to managing those negative thought patterns and learning new ways to deal with them. It won’t be an overnight fix, but with time and practice, you can start to see things in a more balanced way.”

Maya thought about it for a moment. The idea of being able to change how she saw the world felt almost impossible, but also like something she desperately needed. Maybe, just maybe, it was worth a try.

“Okay,” she said quietly, “I’m willing to give it a try.”

---

Over the next few weeks, Maya began learning the core skills of DBT. The first was mindfulness—the practice of staying present and aware in the moment, instead of letting her thoughts carry her away. It was hard at first; her mind always wanted to race ahead, to spiral into worry and doubt. But with practice, she began to notice when she was getting caught up in her thoughts. She learned to pause, to breathe, and to remind herself that not everything needed to be judged or fixed in that very moment.

Dr. Peterson also worked with her on identifying her cognitive distortions. Whenever Maya caught herself thinking in extremes, she would write it down. “I failed the presentation today,” she might write, followed by the distortion: *All-or-nothing thinking.* Then, she would challenge that thought—*I made some mistakes, but I also had some good moments. I can improve next time.*

At first, it felt like a slow and awkward process. Her thoughts didn’t change overnight, and there were times when she still felt overwhelmed by self-doubt. But Maya noticed something small—she was beginning to catch herself in those negative thoughts more quickly. And when she did, she could pause and remind herself that not everything was as bad as it seemed.

Over time, she noticed that she wasn’t catastrophizing as much. The little setbacks didn’t feel like signs of total failure anymore. And, for the first time in a long time, she started to see herself with more compassion, instead of through the distorted lens of her inner critic.

By the end of the program, Maya could look back and recognize the small victories along the way. She wasn’t “fixed,” but she had learned how to handle her thoughts with more clarity and control. And that, she realized, was enough for now. 

As she left Dr. Peterson’s office after one of their final sessions, she felt a new sense of possibility. Maybe, just maybe, the world didn’t have to be as dark and overwhelming as she’d once believed. With DBT, she was learning to see it differently—through a clearer lens.
