**Title: Through the Window**

Sophia sat across from Dr. Emerson in the quiet, dimly lit office. The walls were a soft gray, adorned with paintings of serene landscapes that felt distant to her. She felt like an outsider in this room, like she didn’t belong here. The ticking of the clock on the wall sounded too loud, and the air around her seemed to grow thicker with every passing second.

Dr. Emerson was in her mid-forties, her glasses perched low on her nose, a pen in hand, as she reviewed a notepad. Sophia couldn’t help but wonder what the doctor was writing. Was she making notes about Sophia’s behavior? Or perhaps she was assessing the situation, trying to decide what exactly was wrong with her?

It had been weeks since Sophia had agreed to this evaluation, after her therapist, Anna, suggested it. The idea of being labeled, of finally putting a name to the chaos she’d felt her entire life, made her uneasy. But Anna had insisted. “It’s a tool for understanding yourself,” she’d said. Sophia didn’t know if she believed that. She wasn’t sure she *wanted* to understand herself any better. Some parts of her were easier to ignore.

Dr. Emerson broke the silence, her voice calm but direct. “Sophia, I’ve reviewed the information you provided me, and now we’re going to discuss some things. I’m going to ask you a series of questions about how you’ve been feeling and how you see yourself, okay?”

Sophia nodded. She had rehearsed this moment a dozen times, thinking about what she might say. What would the doctor want to hear? What would make her look like she was trying too hard? There was no script for this. It was all so personal.

“Let’s start with relationships,” Dr. Emerson began, her tone even but probing. “How would you describe your relationships with others? Are they stable, or do you notice frequent changes?”

Sophia hesitated, her hands twisting in her lap. "They... they change a lot. One minute, everything feels fine, and then the next, I feel like I’m being abandoned. People leave, or they say I’m too much. I get scared. I get angry. I don’t know why, but it just feels like I’m losing them every time."

Dr. Emerson nodded, her expression unreadable. “I hear that a lot from people who struggle with intense emotional reactions. Have you ever noticed patterns in those feelings? For example, do you sometimes feel like the people you care about are either completely good or completely bad?”

Sophia bit her lip, a knot forming in her throat. “Yes,” she admitted, her voice cracking slightly. “When someone hurts me, it feels like they’ve ruined everything. I can’t see the good in them anymore. I can’t forgive them. It’s like... it’s all or nothing.”

Dr. Emerson made a note, but didn’t speak for a moment. She was giving Sophia time to absorb the weight of her own words. Sophia knew what was coming next. She could feel it in her bones, like the ground had shifted beneath her feet.

“Have you ever felt empty, like something is missing inside you, but you don’t know what it is?” Dr. Emerson asked gently.

Sophia looked down at her hands, tracing the lines in her palms, as though the answers were hidden there. “I always feel... like I’m not enough. Like I’m incomplete. No matter how much I try to fill it up, it’s never enough. I get caught in this cycle of trying, and it’s exhausting.”

The doctor leaned forward slightly. “What do you do when you feel that emptiness? How do you cope?”

Sophia’s eyes darted around the room, looking for something to distract her. “I... sometimes I just push people away. I’ve tried to numb it with drinking, or I go online and talk to strangers. It’s not healthy, I know. But it’s easier than sitting with it.”

Dr. Emerson wrote something down, and for a brief moment, Sophia thought she saw a flicker of concern in the doctor’s eyes. But it disappeared as quickly as it had come. “It’s very common for people to look for ways to numb the pain. You’re not alone in that.”

Sophia felt a twinge of relief, as if for the first time in her life, someone truly understood the constant, gnawing emptiness she carried. But the relief was fleeting. The diagnosis, the evaluation—did it mean that something was wrong with her? Something that couldn’t be fixed?

Dr. Emerson continued. “One last question, Sophia. Have you ever had difficulty controlling your impulses, especially when you’re upset?”

Sophia’s heart began to race. She had lived her life on a razor’s edge, torn between moments of intense anger, impulsive decisions, and the shame that followed. She had driven too fast, spent money she didn’t have, and lashed out at people she loved. The cycle was endless, and she couldn’t stop it.

“I... I do things sometimes,” she whispered. “Things that scare me. Things I regret right after. But when it happens, it’s like I can’t stop. It feels like... like I’m going to fall apart if I don’t do something.”

Dr. Emerson didn’t react, but Sophia could tell the question had hit closer to home than she had intended. She wasn’t sure what to expect. She’d already been living with so many labels in her head—unstable, broken, unpredictable—but this was different. This was real. This was the moment that would define her.

After a long pause, Dr. Emerson set her pen down. “Thank you for sharing that, Sophia. You’ve been very open today. Based on what you’ve shared, it’s possible that you may be experiencing symptoms of Borderline Personality Disorder. But this is just one part of the picture, and we’ll need more time to explore your experiences fully. This diagnosis isn’t the end, but rather the beginning of a journey toward understanding and healing.”

Sophia felt a lump in her throat. BPD. Borderline Personality Disorder. She had heard about it before—heard the stories of people who had it, the stigma, the misunderstanding. Could that be her? Was she one of those people?

Dr. Emerson gave her a reassuring smile. “We’ll take this one step at a time, Sophia. There’s hope, and there’s support. You don’t have to go through this alone.”

Sophia looked out the window, the gray sky outside reflecting her thoughts. She didn’t know where the road would lead from here, but for the first time in a long time, she felt the faintest flicker of possibility. Maybe, just maybe, she could begin to piece herself together.

**Title: The Blank Canvas**

James had always struggled with his imagination. It wasn’t that he didn’t have ideas or couldn’t think creatively—he was a graphic designer, after all. But when his friend Sarah asked him to close his eyes and picture a beach, he was always perplexed. "I just... can’t," he’d admitted, shrugging it off. "It’s just not something I do." Sarah, being the daydreamer she was, had been shocked. “But you don’t even see it in your head?” she asked, a mix of curiosity and disbelief in her voice. "Like, you don’t get any images at all?"

James had laughed, but something about the question lingered in his mind. For years, he'd thought everyone thought like he did—having an idea, a feeling, a description of things, but never seeing them clearly in their mind’s eye. The concept of “visualizing” something had always seemed abstract to him, almost as if people were speaking a different language. He could read a book and understand the story, but if someone asked him to picture the characters or settings, he was always drawing blanks. 

So, when his therapist, Dr. Hayes, suggested that he might be experiencing something called *aphantasia*—the inability to voluntarily picture images in one's mind—it felt like someone had turned on a light in a dark room. It was strange, almost a little unnerving. He’d never even known there was a term for what he had experienced his entire life.

Now, sitting in Dr. Hayes’ office for an evaluation, James fidgeted nervously. The walls were lined with bookshelves, the air smelled faintly of lavender and paper. It was cozy, yet he felt out of place, as though he were an actor who didn’t fully understand the script. Dr. Hayes, a woman in her mid-thirties with round glasses and an easy smile, looked at him kindly. She’d been his therapist for months now, but today felt different. Today, they were going to talk about something James had never really thought of as an issue—his lack of mental imagery.

“James,” Dr. Hayes began gently, “I want you to try an exercise. Close your eyes, and picture a rose. Just think about it. What do you see?”

James blinked, feeling the familiar blankness settle in. It wasn’t frustration—he wasn’t upset by it anymore, though he used to be—but more of a quiet resignation. He closed his eyes anyway, focusing on the task. He tried to conjure the image of a rose. Its petals, its deep red color, the way it might look in the sun. But the only thing that came to mind was a vague, undefined shape, not much more than an outline. He couldn’t see anything else. 

“I… I can’t see it,” he said, feeling embarrassed, though he had said this exact sentence more times than he could count. “I know what a rose is, but I can’t picture it. Not in my head.”

Dr. Hayes nodded thoughtfully, scribbling something down on her notepad. “That’s okay, James. It’s actually quite common for people with aphantasia. Do you ever have a sense of *knowing* what something looks like, even if you can’t actually visualize it?”

James thought about it. “Yeah, I guess. Like when I design something, I know what it should look like. I understand the concept, but it’s not like I can see it clearly in my head. It's more like... an abstract understanding, but without any pictures.” He paused. “It’s hard to explain.”

“That's a great description,” Dr. Hayes said. “Many people with aphantasia report that they can’t see mental images, but they still have a strong conceptual understanding of things. They know what they’re thinking about, but it’s not in the form of a picture.”

James felt a little relief at hearing this. He hadn’t realized before that there were others who experienced the same thing. But at the same time, a wave of confusion settled over him. He had always lived this way. Was something wrong with him? Why was this only being brought up now?

Dr. Hayes continued, noticing his pensive expression. “I want to try one more thing. Let’s do a memory exercise. Think back to a time when you were at a family gathering. Close your eyes and try to remember what the room looked like. The people. The decorations. What stands out in that memory?”

James tried, but the mental space in his mind was still just as empty as before. He could recall the event, the sounds, the feelings of being with family. He could even remember what the day had felt like. But the visual aspect? Nothing. There was no vivid picture in his mind. He could remember the colors, the actions, but not in any tangible, mental snapshot.

“It’s just... foggy,” he said, opening his eyes, a little more frustrated now. “I can remember things like what people said, what happened, but the images aren’t there. It’s like the whole thing is just a blur.”

Dr. Hayes smiled gently, writing more notes. “That’s very consistent with aphantasia. People with this condition often have strong memories of events, but the images or visual aspects of those memories don’t come through as clearly. You might feel the emotions, remember the conversations, but the images are often not there.”

James nodded slowly. He had always found it odd when people would talk about their memories in such detail, remembering everything vividly as if they could replay the scene in their heads like a movie. It sounded so strange to him. A part of him had always wondered why he didn’t experience things the same way. 

Dr. Hayes put her pen down and looked at him with understanding. “James, from everything you've described, I believe it's possible that you have aphantasia. It’s not something that’s necessarily a problem or a disorder—it’s simply how your brain processes information. For many people, it can feel isolating at first, but it’s just a different way of experiencing the world. You’re not alone in it.”

James felt a mixture of emotions. Relief, confusion, and even a little bit of wonder. It wasn’t a condition he needed to ‘fix’ or something that was wrong with him, but it was an explanation. It made sense of the quiet void in his head when others were so eager to talk about the vivid images they saw when they closed their eyes.

“I guess I’ve always felt a little disconnected from the way others think about their memories,” James admitted quietly. “But hearing that there’s a name for it... it feels a little less strange.”

Dr. Hayes smiled. “I’m glad it helps. You may not have the vivid mental imagery that others do, but that doesn’t mean your experiences are any less real or important. We all experience the world in different ways.”

As James left the office, he felt the weight of a new understanding settle over him. Aphantasia. He hadn’t known it was even possible to experience the world this way. But now that he had a name for it, it was as though a piece of the puzzle had clicked into place. Maybe, he thought, it wasn’t about filling the blank canvas in his mind with pictures. Maybe it was about learning to appreciate the abstract, the intangible. After all, he could still create beautiful things—without ever needing to see them first.
