The hospital gown was too big, draping over her small frame, but she didn’t mind. For the first time in her life, she felt like she fit inside her own skin.  

The pain was manageable, though the tightness of the binder she had to wear post-op made her breath shallow. Dr. Nguyen had warned her about that—"It’s normal," they’d reassured her. "Your body needs time." Riley nodded back then, but now, sitting in the recovery room, she understood. Healing wasn’t just physical.  

Her girlfriend, Sam, walked in holding a Styrofoam cup of bad hospital coffee. "Nurse says you can go home soon," she said, sitting on the edge of Riley’s bed. "How are you feeling?"  

Riley exhaled carefully. "Like I can finally breathe."  

Sam grinned. "That’s the morphine talking."  

They both laughed, though Riley winced at the movement. She was sore, but even that felt like proof—proof of what she’d done, proof of what she’d survived. The consultations, the insurance battles, the letters from therapists, the waiting lists—every single obstacle had led to this moment.  

A nurse came in to go over post-op care: how to change dressings, signs of infection, when to start gentle stretching. Riley listened carefully, nodding, but she was already imagining what it would be like to shower without dysphoria creeping in, to wear a tank top without layering, to simply exist without that constant discomfort.  

Later, at home, Sam helped her into bed, propping pillows around her so she wouldn’t roll in her sleep. "Tomorrow," she said, brushing a kiss against Riley’s forehead, "we’ll take it slow."  

Riley smiled. Tomorrow was the first day of the rest of her life.

"How are you feeling?" Dr. Patel asked, flipping through her chart during the post-op checkup.  

Ava hesitated, not because she didn’t know the answer, but because words felt too small. "Lighter," she finally said. "Like I can finally see myself."  

Dr. Patel smiled. "That’s what we like to hear." They carefully unwrapped the compression bandages, revealing Ava’s swollen but unmistakably feminine chest. The sight made her throat tighten.  

She had spent years imagining herself in the mirror, trying to superimpose a version of herself that matched how she felt inside. And now, for the first time, she wouldn’t have to pretend.  

"The swelling will go down over the next few weeks," Dr. Patel explained, gently inspecting the incisions. "You’re healing well, but take it easy. No heavy lifting, and be mindful of your posture. Your body’s adjusting."  

Ava barely heard the instructions, too mesmerized by the reflection in the exam room mirror. She wanted to cry but knew the swelling around her eyes from surgery would make it uncomfortable. Instead, she just breathed.  

Later that night, curled up on the couch with her best friend Maya, Ava ran her fingers along the edges of her loose pajama top, marveling at the unfamiliar yet right sensation of fabric against her chest.  

Maya nudged her. "You okay?"
