Dr. Evelyn Carter adjusted her glasses as she flipped through the file of her next patient. **Michael Reynolds, 42, persistent lymphadenopathy**—that’s what the notes said. The referral from his primary doctor mentioned swollen lymph nodes in his neck for over six weeks, no sign of infection, and unintentional weight loss. That last part made her pause.  

She took a breath and pushed open the exam room door.  

Michael sat on the edge of the table, his hands clasped tightly in his lap. He looked tired—dark circles under his eyes, his once-fit frame noticeably thinner.  

“Good afternoon, Mr. Reynolds. I’m Dr. Carter.” She offered a reassuring smile as she pulled up a stool. “I understand you’ve had some swollen lymph nodes?”  

“Yeah,” Michael nodded, his fingers tapping nervously against his knee. “Started a couple of months ago. Thought it was just a cold at first, but it never went away.”  

Dr. Carter nodded, setting her file aside. “Any fever, night sweats, or fatigue?”  

Michael hesitated before answering. “A little bit. I sweat a lot at night… and I’ve been more tired than usual. I figured it was just work stress.”  

She didn’t miss the way he avoided eye contact. Gently, she said, “I’d like to take a look.”  

Michael nodded, pulling down the collar of his shirt. She pressed her fingers gently along the sides of his neck, feeling the firm, swollen nodes. She moved to his underarms, then down to his groin—same result.  

“Have you had any recent infections? Sore throat, dental issues, anything like that?”  

“No, nothing,” he said. “I mean, I had a bad flu last year, but I got over it.”  

Dr. Carter sat back, thoughtful. “Swollen lymph nodes can happen for a lot of reasons, some harmless, some more serious. Because yours have been persistent, and given your weight loss, I’d like to run some tests.”  

Michael’s throat bobbed as he swallowed. “What kind of tests?”  

“We’ll start with some blood work—check for infections, immune disorders, and some other possibilities.” She hesitated, then decided to be direct. “We may also need a biopsy of one of the lymph nodes.”  

Michael stiffened slightly. “You think it’s… cancer?”  

Dr. Carter met his eyes, steady and calm. “I don’t want to jump to conclusions. There are many possible explanations, but I want to make sure we rule out anything serious.”  

A long silence stretched between them. Finally, Michael exhaled, rubbing his face. “Okay. Let’s do it.”  

Dr. Carter patted his shoulder reassuringly. “Whatever it is, we’ll figure it out together.”  

As she left the room to order the tests, she hoped—for his sake—that the truth waiting in the results was a kind one.

Dr. Rachel Lin washed her hands methodically, listening to the rhythmic drip of the faucet as she prepared for her next patient. The chart in front of her read:  

**Name:** Daniel Morris, 55  
**Complaint:** Abdominal bloating, discomfort, and occasional nausea  
**History:** No prior GI conditions, mild hypertension  

Rachel pushed open the exam room door with a practiced smile. "Good afternoon, Mr. Morris."  

Daniel sat on the edge of the exam table, his hands resting on his rounded stomach. He looked uneasy, shifting slightly as he gave a small nod. "Afternoon, doc."  

Rachel pulled up a stool and sat across from him. "So, I see you're experiencing some bloating and discomfort?"  

"Yeah," Daniel sighed. "It started a few weeks ago—at first, I thought it was just something I ate. But it’s not going away. Feels like I’m carrying around a balloon."  

Rachel nodded. "Any pain? Nausea? Changes in bowel movements?"  

He hesitated. "Not really pain, just pressure. And my stomach feels tight. Sometimes I get a little nauseous, but I haven’t thrown up."  

Rachel reached for her stethoscope. "Alright, let’s take a look."  

Daniel lay back on the table, adjusting his shirt so she could access his abdomen. She first placed the stethoscope on his stomach, listening carefully. The gurgling sounds of peristalsis were faint—quieter than normal.  

Next came percussion. She flattened her left hand against his abdomen and used two fingers of her right hand to deliver a firm tap.  

*Thump.*  

She moved her hand slightly and tapped again.  

*Thump…*  

She kept going, moving systematically across the quadrants of his abdomen, listening for differences in tone. Over the upper left, near the stomach, the sound was slightly hollow—normal. But over the central and lower right area—  

*Thump… Thump…*  

Rachel frowned. The sound was more resonant than she expected, almost drum-like.  

She moved toward the flanks and repeated the motion. The pitch changed slightly when he shifted. Fluid? Gas distension? She kept her expression neutral, but her mind was already considering possibilities—bowel obstruction, ascites, excessive gas.  

Daniel must have noticed her hesitation. "What is it?" he asked, his voice cautious.  

Rachel smiled gently. "It looks like you have quite a bit of trapped air, possibly some fluid retention. We’ll need some imaging to get a clearer picture, but the percussion helps us understand what's going on inside."  

He exhaled, rubbing his stomach absentmindedly. "That sounds… not great."  

"Not necessarily bad, either," Rachel reassured him. "Let’s get some tests done and figure it out together."  

As she helped him sit back up, she thought about the hollow echoes beneath her fingers, hoping the answers they revealed would be benign.

Anna Greene sat in the cold exam room, staring at the white walls as the clock ticked too loudly. She was dressed in a thin hospital gown, her arms folded tightly across her chest. The spot where they’d found the lump was just below her left breast—small, firm, unexpected.  

She swallowed, shifting slightly on the crinkled paper of the exam table.  

Dr. Patel entered, her expression calm and reassuring. “Anna,” she said gently, “how are you feeling?”  

Anna forced a small smile. “Just ready to get this over with.”  

Dr. Patel nodded. “Completely understandable. Let’s go over everything one more time before we begin.” She pulled up a stool and met Anna’s eyes. “The imaging showed a mass about a centimeter in size. It could be a benign cyst, fibroadenoma, or something else. A biopsy will give us a clearer answer.”  

Anna nodded, her fingers gripping the edge of the gown. “And this is a core needle biopsy?”  

“Yes,” Dr. Patel confirmed. “We’ll numb the area, then use a small, hollow needle to take tissue samples. You might feel some pressure, but it shouldn’t be painful.”  

Anna took a slow breath. “Okay. Let’s do it.”  

Dr. Patel gave a reassuring smile and began. The nurse cleaned Anna’s skin with antiseptic, the cool sensation making her shiver slightly. A small pinch—local anesthesia. It stung for only a second before fading into numbness.  

Dr. Patel positioned the ultrasound probe over the lump. “Alright, Anna. You might hear a clicking sound when we take the samples.”  

Anna nodded, watching on the monitor as the dark shape of the lump came into view.  

A slight pressure. Then—*click.*  

The needle withdrew and re-entered. *Click.*  

Anna exhaled slowly, focusing on the rhythm of her breath.  

“One more,” Dr. Patel murmured. *Click.*  

And just like that, it was over. The nurse pressed a gauze pad to the biopsy site, applying gentle pressure before securing it with a bandage.  

“All done,” Dr. Patel said, peeling off her gloves. “You did great.”  

Anna let out a breath she hadn’t realized she was holding. “That was… quicker than I expected.”  

Dr. Patel nodded. “Most of the discomfort is from the anticipation. You might be sore for a day or two, but ice packs and Tylenol will help.” She softened her voice. “We’ll have the results in a few days.”  

Anna managed a nod, her fingers tracing the bandage absently. A few days.  

Dr. Patel reached out, giving her shoulder a reassuring squeeze. “No matter what, we’ll take this one step at a time.”  

Anna gave a small, grateful smile. She wasn’t sure how she’d get through the waiting—but for now, she was one step closer to an answer.

Nathan Pierce sat in the dimly lit exam room, blinking as his pupils struggled to adjust. His eyes felt strange—artificially wide from the dilation drops, the cool air making them water. He wiped a tear from the corner of his eye and glanced at the machine in front of him.  

“So, this is the OCT thing?” he asked.  

Dr. Ellis, the ophthalmologist, smiled as she adjusted the settings on the device. “That’s right. Optical Coherence Tomography. It’s a non-invasive scan that gives us a detailed, cross-sectional image of your retina.”  

Nathan nodded slowly. He hadn’t thought much about his eyes before—not until he started seeing strange flashes in the corner of his vision last week. Then came the blurry spot, just off-center in his left eye. It wasn’t huge, but it was enough to make him nervous.  

“Go ahead and rest your chin on the support,” Dr. Ellis instructed. “Forehead against the bar.”  

Nathan leaned forward, positioning himself as directed. The machine hummed softly.  

“Okay, I need you to keep your eye open and focus on the green light inside.”  

He peered in, his gaze locking onto the tiny glowing dot. A circular pattern of red and blue lights swirled around it, like something out of a sci-fi movie.  

A faint *whirr* filled the room as the machine scanned his retina.  

“Doing great,” Dr. Ellis said. “Try not to blink for just a few more seconds.”  

Nathan’s eye burned with the effort, but he forced himself to keep staring. Another faint whirr, another sweep of the scanner.  

“Alright, you can sit back now,” she said.  

Nathan blinked rapidly, his vision still hazy from the bright light. “That’s it?”  

“That’s it.” She turned to the screen, analyzing the freshly captured images. His retina appeared in fine layers, like the rings of a tree.  

She frowned slightly, zooming in. Nathan felt a knot tighten in his stomach. “Is… something wrong?”  

Dr. Ellis hesitated, then turned to him with a measured look. “I see some swelling in your macula. It could be central serous retinopathy, which is often stress-related and temporary. But I want to be sure, so I’ll compare this to your other eye and run a few more tests.”  

Nathan exhaled slowly. Stress. That sounded about right.  

Dr. Ellis smiled reassuringly. “No need to panic yet. We’re catching this early, and that’s the most important thing.”  

Nathan nodded, rubbing his palms against his jeans. He had walked in afraid of the unknown. Now, at least, he had something to hold onto—a first glimpse into the hidden world inside his own eyes.

James Carter sat in the audiologist’s office, tapping his fingers against his knee. The room was quiet, too quiet—ironic, considering why he was there.  

For months, he’d noticed it. Conversations felt muffled, as if people were speaking through a thick pane of glass. He had started asking *What?* more often. His wife, Emily, teased him for turning the TV up too loud. At first, he shrugged it off. But then he missed the sound of his granddaughter giggling across the room. That was when he knew—something was wrong.  

“Mr. Carter?”  

He looked up as Dr. Rosen, the audiologist, entered with a warm smile. “Are you ready for your hearing test?”  

“As ready as I’ll ever be,” James muttered.  

Dr. Rosen led him to a small, soundproof booth with a chair and a pair of heavy headphones. “This is a pure-tone audiometry test,” she explained. “I’ll play sounds at different pitches and volumes. Raise your hand whenever you hear something, even if it’s faint.”  

James nodded and settled into the chair as she placed the headphones over his ears. The door closed with a soft *click*, sealing him in. The silence felt thicker now.  

A tone played in his right ear—clear and sharp. He raised his hand immediately.  

Another tone, slightly softer. He raised his hand again.  

Then, a much fainter sound. He hesitated. Was that real, or just his mind filling the silence? He raised his hand anyway.  

The tones continued, moving between his left and right ear. Some were easy to hear. Others… not so much. When Dr. Rosen switched to speech testing, James strained to make out the words filtering through the headphones.  

“Say the word *baseball*.”  

“Baseball,” he repeated.  

“Say the word *door*.”  

“…Four?”  

The recording moved on, but James swallowed hard. He had misheard it.  

The test ended, and the door reopened. Dr. Rosen gestured for him to return to her desk. She studied the audiogram results, her expression neutral but thoughtful.  

James clenched his fists. “Well?”  

She turned the monitor toward him, pointing at the graph. “You have mild to moderate hearing loss, especially in the higher frequencies. This explains why speech has started to sound muffled.”  

James exhaled. “So… I need hearing aids?”  

Dr. Rosen smiled gently. “They’d definitely help. But the good news is that you came in early. You’re not alone in this, James.”  

He nodded slowly, rubbing his temples. He had feared the answer, but somehow, knowing was better than guessing.  

As he stepped out of the office, the world sounded the same as before—but now, he understood what he had been missing.
